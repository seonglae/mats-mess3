\section{Mess3 Transition Matrices}
\label{app:mess3}

With $\beta = (1-\alpha)/2$ and $y = 1-2x$:
\begin{align}
T^{(0)} &= \begin{pmatrix} \alpha y & \beta x & \beta x \\ \alpha x & \beta y & \beta x \\ \alpha x & \beta x & \beta y \end{pmatrix} \\
T^{(1)} &= \begin{pmatrix} \beta y & \alpha x & \beta x \\ \beta x & \alpha y & \beta x \\ \beta x & \alpha x & \beta y \end{pmatrix} \\
T^{(2)} &= \begin{pmatrix} \beta y & \beta x & \alpha x \\ \beta x & \beta y & \alpha x \\ \beta x & \beta x & \alpha y \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}

The net transition matrix is:
\begin{equation}
T = T^{(0)} + T^{(1)} + T^{(2)} = \begin{pmatrix} 1-2x & x & x \\ x & 1-2x & x \\ x & x & 1-2x \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
with uniform stationary distribution $\bpi = [1/3, 1/3, 1/3]$ and non-trivial eigenvalue $\zeta = 1 - 3x$.

The emission probability $P(z \mid s) = \sum_j T^{(z)}_{sj}$ gives the entropy rate:
\begin{equation}
H = -\sum_{s=1}^{3} \frac{1}{3} \sum_{z=0}^{2} P(z|s) \log P(z|s)
\end{equation}

\section{Bayes-Optimal Loss Derivation}
\label{app:bayes}

The Bayes-optimal loss at position $t$ is the cross-entropy achieved by a predictor maintaining exact belief states:
\begin{equation}
\text{CE}(t) = \E_{z_{1:t}} \left[ -\log P(z_t \mid z_{1:t-1}) \right]
\end{equation}
where $P(z_t \mid z_{1:t-1}) = \veta^{(z_{1:t-1})} T^{(z_t)} \mathbf{1}$.

For the non-ergodic mixture, we compute two baselines:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Oracle}: knows the component, uses component-specific transition matrices.
    \item \textbf{Bayesian}: must infer the component, uses the mixture prediction $P(z_t \mid z_{1:t-1}) = \sum_k P(k \mid z_{1:t-1}) \cdot P(z_t \mid z_{1:t-1}, k)$.
\end{itemize}
The gap between oracle and Bayesian baselines quantifies the cost of not knowing the component identity.
